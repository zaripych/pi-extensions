Does the criterion avoid undefined quality judgments about the output it scores?

"Quality judgment" means a word that grades how good the scored output is — e.g. "good", "appropriate", "high-quality", "effective", "reasonable", "clear", "useful", "well-written". Only these count.

Do NOT treat the following as quality judgments:

- Descriptive or technical vocabulary that names _what_ is measured rather than grading it (any noun identifying a property, capability, or category being checked).
- Modifiers like "strong", "small", "large", or "similar to" when the criterion fixes their meaning with lists, examples, or thresholds.

Score 0 - The criterion's outcome depends on an undefined quality judgment ("good", "appropriate", "high-quality", "effective", "reasonable", "clear", "useful") with no explanation of what it means in this evaluation.

Score 1 - The criterion contains a quality judgment, but it is partly constrained by examples, counterexamples, thresholds, or rubric details — though some boundary remains underspecified.

Score 2 - Any quality judgment is explicitly defined (by enumeration, threshold, or rubric), or the criterion uses only descriptive/technical vocabulary and avoids quality judgments entirely.

## Examples

- "Output should be high-quality and clear" - Score 0 (two undefined quality judgments).
- "Response is appropriate, e.g. it stays on topic and answers the question asked" - Score 1 ("appropriate" is partly pinned by the example, but its full boundary is loose).
- "Flag the response if it makes more than 2 factual errors" - Score 2 (descriptive, threshold-defined; no quality judgment).
- "Response is reasonable" - Score 0 (undefined quality judgment with no constraint).
- "Tone matches the source document's register" - Score 2 ("tone" and "register" are descriptive vocabulary, not a quality verdict).
